Research

Every year, nearly 200,000 people in the US die from traumatic
injuries — car crashes, falls, workplace accidents, violence — and the
number-one cause of preventable death is blood loss. USAMRDC
Hemorrhage accounts for 30–40% of all trauma deaths, and of those,
33–56% happen before the patient ever reaches a hospital. Lippincott
Williams & Wilkins A person with life-threatening hemorrhage can lose
their entire blood volume in under five minutes PubMed Central, but
median EMS arrival time is six minutes, with definitive care taking a
median of 60 minutes PubMed Central — meaning the bleed-out clock
often expires before anyone with the right tools arrives. The
intervention itself is not complex: in a landmark military study
(Kragh et al., Annals of Surgery, 2009), tourniquets applied before
the onset of shock were associated with 90% survival versus just 10%
when applied after shock had set in PubMed, and no limbs were lost due
to tourniquet use PubMed — yet in a 10-year civilian study at Boston
Medical Center, only 2% of patients with qualifying injuries had a
tourniquet applied before reaching the hospital. EMS1 The problem
isn't skill or willingness — it's that bystanders simply don't have
the tools. Meanwhile, a clinical trial in Sweden proved that
autonomous drones delivered medical devices before ambulance arrival
in two-thirds of cases, with a median time benefit exceeding three
minutes Interhospi — but that system delivers defibrillators for
cardiac arrest, not hemorrhage kits. Our project bridges that gap:
drone-delivered bleeding control kits paired with real-time bystander
coaching, designed to put a tourniquet in a civilian's hands within
the minutes that the data shows separate survival from death.
